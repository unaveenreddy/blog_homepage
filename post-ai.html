What We Get Wrong About AI

May 20, 2026 · 8 min read

Artificial intelligence is everywhere — and so is the noise around it. Hype cycles come and go, but the conversation around AI is especially prone to extremes. Either it will save us all, or it will replace us all. The truth is far more mundane — and far more interesting.

AI Is Not Magic

Behind every impressive output is a statistical model trained on vast datasets. Large language models don't "understand" language the way humans do — they predict the next most probable token based on patterns. That's powerful, but it's not sentience.

It Won't Replace You (Yet)

AI excels at narrow, repetitive tasks. It can draft emails, generate code snippets, and summarise documents. But it struggles with nuance, context, creativity, and empathy. The jobs most at risk aren't white-collar roles — they're tasks, not careers.

The real risk isn't that AI will become too smart. It's that we'll trust it for things it was never designed to do.

Bias Is a Feature, Not a Bug

Models trained on human data inherit human biases. If your training data over-represents certain demographics, the model will too. This isn't a flaw that can be patched away — it requires ongoing vigilance, diverse datasets, and ethical oversight.

What We Should Focus On

Instead of asking whether AI will take our jobs, we should ask how we build systems that augment human capability. The best outcomes come from human-in-the-loop design: AI handles the grunt work, humans handle the judgment calls.

The future of AI isn't about machines becoming human. It's about humans using better tools to make better decisions.
